# S&T Desk-by-Desk Prep Sheet
### Framework: (1) key debate → (2) Side A vs Side B + who's on each + hardest call → (3) what resolves it and how it trades into resolution
*Compiled July 8, 2026. Skim the tape the morning of the event — a CPI print or Fed-speak can shift any of these overnight.*

**How to use this:** Don't recite. Pick the debate for whoever you're talking to, ask "what's your take on X," then follow up on their answer. The framework is so you can *hold the conversation*, not script it. Your best line at any desk: "which side of [debate] is your flow on?" — it asks about positioning, which is what traders actually know.

## THE FOUR HUBS
Almost every desk's debate is a spoke off one of these. Internalize the hubs and you can reconstruct any desk's version live.

**Hub 1 — The Warsh Fed: hike vs. hold.** June FOMC (Warsh's first as Chair) held at 3.50–3.75%, removed the easing bias, and scrapped traditional forward guidance. The dot plot split almost perfectly: 9 officials see at least one hike this year, 9 see no move or a cut. May CPI printed 4.2% y/y — a 3-year high — driven by the war's energy shock. The question the whole floor is arguing: does the Fed look through a supply shock, or has inflation psychology broken?

**Hub 2 — The Iran war and its fragile peace.** The Feb 28 U.S./Israel–Iran war closed the Strait of Hormuz (~20–27% of seaborne oil, ~20% of global LNG), Iran hit Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex (−17% of Qatari capacity, 3–5 years to repair), and the IEA ran its largest-ever emergency stock release. A provisional/interim peace deal has Brent back near $70 (from a >$100 spike), but implementation risk is the live question: is the premium gone for good, or one headline from coming back?

**Hub 3 — The AI supercycle.** Data-center buildout is pulling on everything: power demand (+~4%/yr globally), copper, silver, credit issuance to fund capex, and now equity supply — SpaceX listed June 12 (popped ~20%, then slid from >$225 to ~$153), Anthropic filed confidentially June 1, OpenAI filed about a week later. Estimated $100–200bn of combined AI/space IPO demand vs. $28.4bn of total US IPO proceeds YTD. The question: can public markets absorb it, and what gets sold to fund it?

**Hub 4 — Crowding and leverage in the fund complex.** HF gross leverage is elevated; medium-term momentum is crowded and has driven a big share of equity L/S alpha YTD (Man Group's warning). March 2026 delivered the live demo: a sharp factor rotation forced pod de-grossing and losses, then April was the best HF month in a decade as dispersion returned. Everyone is now arguing about whether the next de-gross is a buying opportunity or the big one.

## RATES — full curve

### Front end (2s and in): hike vs. hold
**Debate:** Is the next move a hike? Markets price roughly 80%+ odds of a July 28–29 hold, but ~20-30% hike odds have flickered; nine dots want a hike this year.
**Side A (hike / hawks):** BofA moved its base case to *three* 25bp hikes in 2026 citing sticky inflation and Warsh's hawkish debut tone. Fast money paying fixed, positioning for the front end to reprice.
**Side B (hold):** JPM says the market over-reacted hawkishly and sees a hold all year; Goldman pushed first *cuts* to June/Dec 2027 but argues the Fed historically doesn't hike into oil shocks it deems transient. Real money receiving on backups. Note the Fed's own doves: Waller (and at times Bowman) have dissented *for cuts* on labor-market softness — the committee itself is the two sides.
**Hardest call:** Classifying an energy-driven CPI print in real time — supply shock to look through vs. inflation-expectations damage to act on. Warsh killed forward guidance, so every meeting is live and there's no hand-holding.
**Resolves on:** July CPI, July 28–29 FOMC, payrolls, whether services inflation broadens beyond energy, Iran-deal durability (oil → CPI passthrough). 
**How it trades:** Front-end vol stays bid into each print; violent repricings both ways; SOFR/fed funds strips whip on every Fed speech because there's no guidance anchor.

### Belly (5s–10s): the range and the growth-scare bid
**Debate:** Is 4.0–4.5% on the 10yr a durable range, and does a growth scare or the inflation impulse break it first? 10s have mostly held that band since March (Schwab's framing); the war adds stagflation risk — energy inflation up, growth forecasts down.
**Side A:** Duration buyers — any real growth wobble (labor cracks, energy demand destruction) pulls 10s below 4% fast; the belly is the hedge that still works.
**Side B:** Sticky 3%+ core inflation plus heavy issuance means backups to the top of the range get sold — bear-steepener camp says the belly is trapped.
**Hardest call:** Owning duration as a recession hedge when the recession scenario itself (oil shock) is *inflationary* — the hedge can fail exactly when you need it.
**Resolves on:** Growth data (claims, ISM, consumer), CPI composition, and whether the Fed's reaction function turns out to prioritize inflation or employment.

### Long end (30s): term premium, deficits, supply
**Debate:** Structural bear steepening vs. mean reversion. Deficit penciled around 6% of GDP for 2026; term premium elevated on fiscal worry, sticky inflation, and softer foreign demand. Continuum-type forecasts see 10s2s steepening toward 90–100bp.
**Side A (steepener — the crowded macro trade):** Fiscal trajectory has no consolidation path; every heavy auction needs a concession; foreign/real-money demand at the long end is thinner and more price-sensitive than it used to be. Pensions/LDI buy dips but at *their* levels.
**Side B (flattener / long-end value):** At ~2% real yields on 10s, longs are historically attractive; the Fed's balance-sheet shift helps — it stopped QT and began buying bills/short coupons (~$40bn/mo pace for reserve management, and passive Treasury holdings growth of maybe $25bn/mo in 2026), which eases net supply vs. other G4 markets where QT continues. If growth cracks, the long end rallies hard from these levels.
**Hardest call:** Auction cycle risk management — how much concession to demand into 30yr supply when a single tail can gap the curve; and whether to fade or follow a fiscal headline.
**Resolves on:** Quarterly refunding announcements, auction tails/stats, term-premium model updates, deficit trajectory (tariff-revenue court rulings are a live wildcard), foreign demand data (TIC).
**How it trades:** Steepeners grind, then gap on supply events; long-end vol elevated relative to history; watch swap spreads for balance-sheet stress.

### Cross-market wrinkle worth knowing
JGB yields at multi-decade highs as the BoJ normalizes (policy rate 0.75%, market pricing more hikes) — a structural drag on global duration demand since Japanese investors were the marginal buyer of global long bonds. Also ECB: it *postponed* planned cuts on March 19 because of the energy shock — the war flipped Europe's central bank hawkish too.
**Question to ask a rates trader:** "Are you treating energy CPI as noise or signal — and is your flow on the same side as you?"

## FX — the most debated USD pairs

### USD/JPY (DM — the main event)
**Debate:** Does the pair break 160 and force intervention, or does BoJ normalization finally crack it lower? Spot has pressed toward the critical 160 zone even *with* the BoJ hiking (0.75%, ~70% odds priced of another hike near-term, possibly 1.25% by year-end) because the oil shock hammers Japan's terms of trade (energy importer) and U.S. yields stay high. Bank forecasts are unusually dispersed — roughly 150 (Scotiabank) to 164 (JPM) — which tells you the street genuinely disagrees.
**Side A (higher / carry-on):** Rate differential still ~300bp; carry trade demand persistent in a contained-vol regime; Japan's structural trade deficit is yen-negative. Systematic/carry money is long.
**Side B (lower / normalization):** BoJ keeps hiking while the Fed is capped; MOF intervention above ~155–160 (2024 precedent: $62bn defended); any risk-off or carry unwind is violent — 500–1,000 pips in days, and it crashes *all* JPY crosses plus correlated equities together.
**Hardest call:** Being long carry into an intervention zone — the MOF cares about *speed* of the move more than level, so the trade works until it instantly doesn't. Position sizing into asymmetric, event-driven risk.
**Resolves on:** BoJ meetings (next hike timing), MOF verbal escalation, U.S. CPI/FOMC, oil (Japan's import bill).
**How it trades:** Grinds higher on carry, gaps lower on intervention/BoJ surprises; JPY vol smile heavily skewed toward yen-strength tails.

### EUR/USD (DM)
**Debate:** How much of an energy tax can Europe absorb? EUR hit multi-month lows as the war re-ran the 2022 playbook: Qatari LNG suspension + 30% storage after a cold winter → TTF doubling → ECB *postpones* cuts, raises 2026 inflation forecast, cuts growth. That's the stagflation mix.
**Side A (short EUR):** Energy-importing economy facing recession risk with a central bank that can't ease into it; U.S. is a net energy exporter — structural USD advantage in this specific shock.
**Side B (long EUR / mean reversion):** Peace-deal implementation + LNG re-routing normalizes gas by winter; ECB hawkishness on inflation actually *narrows* rate differentials; positioning already very short.
**Hardest call:** Trading a pair where both central banks turned hawkish for *different reasons* — rate differential logic gets murky when the driver is a common shock hitting asymmetrically.
**Resolves on:** European gas storage refill pace over the summer (the single best indicator), Qatari LNG restart timeline, ECB meetings, deal headlines.

### USD/BRL (EM — commodity winner leg)
**Debate:** Does Brazil's commodity-exporter windfall beat the strong-dollar/high-U.S.-rates drag? Historically an 800–1,200-pip-range pair on politics and commodity swings.
**Side A (BRL bulls):** High local carry, agriculture and resource export revenues boosted by the supply shock, and Brazil is geographically insulated from Hormuz.
**Side B (BRL bears):** Classic EM math — U.S. higher-for-longer plus global risk-off dominates any commodity tailwind; fiscal/political noise is always one headline away.
**Hardest call:** Carry trades in EM when the funding leg (USD) keeps getting more attractive — the carry is real but so is the drawdown when de-risking hits.
**Resolves on:** Fed path (the dominant input for all EM), commodity prices, Brazilian fiscal headlines.

### USD/MXN (EM)
**Debate:** Peso resilience vs. U.S.-cycle sensitivity. Oil revenue helps Mexico; nearshoring flows are structural support; but MXN is the market's favorite liquid EM proxy — first thing sold in any de-risking.
**Side A:** Carry + oil + nearshoring = keep earning it. **Side B:** MXN's liquidity makes it the hedge vehicle of choice, so it overshoots in every risk event regardless of fundamentals; U.S. trade-policy headlines are a constant tail.
**Resolves on:** Fed, oil, U.S. growth (Mexico's export demand), trade-policy noise.

### Asia energy importers (INR, KRW, PHP and friends)
**Debate:** How much damage does the fuel crisis do? These economies import Gulf energy; the crisis hit fuel availability, current accounts, and FX reserves (Bangladesh-style stress at the extreme; panic buying across parts of Asia).
**Side A:** Central banks defend; oil back near $70 post-deal means the worst is priced. **Side B:** Reserve drawdown + imported inflation + Fed higher-for-longer is the classic EM squeeze; the LNG shortage (Asia spot LNG spiked >140% at the peak) persists into 2027 because Qatari capacity is physically damaged.
**Question to ask an FX trader:** "Is the dollar trading rates or trading risk right now — and does that flip if the Iran deal wobbles?"

## COMMODITIES

### Natural gas / LNG (the war trade)
**State of play:** Hormuz closure took ~20% of global LNG offline; Qatar declared force majeure; the Ras Laffan strike cut Qatari capacity ~17% with a 3–5 year repair. Asian spot LNG spiked >140%; TTF roughly doubled to >€60/MWh against 30% storage; U.S. Henry Hub barely moved (+~7%) because North American supply is the shock absorber. IEA sees a cumulative ~120bcm LNG supply loss 2026–2030 — the "global LNG supply wave" is delayed, not just disrupted.
**Debate:** Is this a one-winter squeeze or a multi-year regime change?
**Side A (structural):** Physical capacity is *damaged*, not just blockaded — this outlasts the peace deal. U.S. LNG buildout accelerates (export capacity set to more than double 2024→2028); U.S. gas gets pulled toward world prices via exports. Long-term buyers locking contracts now.
**Side B (transient):** Demand destruction is already visible (global gas consumption now expected flat in 2026); Europe substitutes with renewables and coal; Asia balks at LNG dependence entirely (several countries rethinking import projects) — the crisis *kills* long-term LNG demand growth rather than rewarding it.
**Hardest call:** Hedging winter 2026–27 European supply when the marginal cargo's availability depends on a ceasefire holding — vega you can't replace if it breaks.
**Resolves on:** European storage refill pace through summer (watch weekly), Qatari restart milestones, deal implementation, U.S. export terminal startups, winter weather.
**How it trades:** Massive geographic basis (US vs TTF vs JKM) — this is a spread trader's market; calendar spreads price the "when does Qatar come back" question directly.

### Gold (Iran + macro)
**State of play:** Blowoff to >$5,400/oz intraday in late January, then a ~17% multi-week correction; found a floor around $4,170–4,400 and has traded a technical no-man's-land between the 200dma (~$4,340) and 50dma (~$4,730). Central banks bought ~900+ tons in 2025; PBoC accelerated again in March–April (1 ton/mo pace → 5, then 8 tons), and Chinese net imports tripled q/q in Q1.
**Debate:** Is the debasement/reserve-diversification bid structural, or does a Fed *hiking* cycle finally crack it?
**Side A (structural bulls):** JPM targets ~$6,000 by end-2026; the driver set — fiscal deficits, sanctions-driven reserve diversification (the 2022 Russia asset freeze changed central-bank behavior permanently), geopolitical fragmentation — doesn't depend on the Fed. Gold rallied through *rising* real yields in 2025, breaking the old model.
**Side B (bears/skeptics):** JPM's own stated bear case — if U.S. growth stays hot and inflation accelerates into a genuine Fed hiking cycle, Western ETF money flips to sustained outflows and the correction extends. Momentum/CTA money already trimmed after the January blowoff.
**Hardest call:** Position sizing an asset whose traditional valuation anchor (real yields) has stopped working — you're trading flows (central banks, ETFs) you can only observe with a lag.
**Resolves on:** Fed path (a hike is the bear catalyst), monthly central-bank purchase data, ETF flow data, Iran-deal durability.

### Copper (AI/HPC + war supply chain)
**State of play:** Record highs — LME touched ~$11,771/t; 2026 consensus just above $12,100/t. But the story is two-sided: *concentrate* is desperately tight (spot treatment charges around −$70/t — smelters effectively paying for feed; ~3Mt cumulative concentrate deficit projected by 2036) while *refined inventories* are elevated (>1.3Mt visible in March). The war even hit the supply chain sideways: Gulf sulfuric-acid exports strangled → China halted its own exports → Chilean processing costs up ~5% (Codelco).
**Debate:** Is the AI/electrification demand story (data centers +15% copper demand impulse, EVs at 60–100kg/unit, grid buildout) enough to justify record prices *now*, or is this early?
**Side A (bulls):** Demand curve steepens every year and supply physically can't respond — most new supply is brownfield; the deficit is a when, not an if. Goldman's own projection of 165% data-center power growth by 2030 is a copper order.
**Side B (Goldman's actual copper view, notably):** Above $11,000 is hard to justify with inventories elevated and no true structural shortage before the back end of the decade — the market is pricing 2030's deficit in 2026.
**Hardest call:** Trading a market where the concentrate leg and the refined leg tell opposite stories — which price is "copper"?
**Resolves on:** Visible inventory draws/builds, TC/RC negotiations, China smelter run-rates, mine disruption headlines (Chile/Peru/Indonesia), data-center capex announcements.

### Silver (AI/HPC + squeeze mechanics)
**State of play:** Up >100% in 2025 through >$60/oz; fifth consecutive year of structural deficit (~95Moz gap); mine supply is inelastic because ~70% of silver is a *byproduct* of other metals — price can't summon supply. Demand: PV (~200Moz+/yr), plus EVs, semis, 5G, and AI data-center electrical load. Citi has floated $110 for H2; Goldman $85–100 average.
**Debate:** Physical squeeze vs. demand rollover. 
**Side A:** Thinning London/Shanghai inventories + inelastic supply + industrial buyers who *must* have it = price discovery with no overhead resistance until far higher; large paper short base adds squeeze fuel.
**Side B:** High prices drive thrifting (PV already uses less silver per unit), recycling supply responds, and an industrial-demand-heavy metal gets hit if global growth rolls; Metals Focus already sees the deficit *narrowing*.
**Hardest call:** Squeeze trades are timing trades — being right on the deficit and wrong on the month is still losing.
**Resolves on:** LBMA/SHFE inventory data, lease rates (the squeeze tell), PV installation data, industrial production.
**Question to ask a commodities trader:** "Has the vol been tradeable or just dangerous — and are you positioned for the deal holding or breaking?"

## CREDIT — by sector, mapped to the megatrends

The three megatrends that cut across every credit sector right now: **(1) AI capex is migrating onto balance sheets** — hyperscaler/data-center buildout increasingly debt-funded (record convertible issuance too: $166bn in 2025 at ~2x the average year, with AI-adjacent share rising into Q4); **(2) the energy shock** creates sharp sector winners/losers and feeds the inflation that keeps refinancing rates high; **(3) higher-for-longer refi math** — the maturity wall gets repriced at 3.50–3.75% base rates plus spread, which is fine for IG and existential for the weak tail of HY.

### TMT / Tech (IG heavyweight)
**Debate:** Is debt-funded AI capex credit-negative? Hyperscalers were cash machines; now they're issuing tens of billions to fund data centers with uncertain ROI timelines.
**Side A:** Balance sheets are fortress-grade, the capex is revenue-generating infrastructure, spreads should stay tight — buy every new deal (and the new-issue concessions on mega-deals are the year's best entry points).
**Side B:** This is the cycle turn in slow motion — capex intensity up, FCF conversion down, and if AI monetization disappoints, today's A-rated issuance is tomorrow's fallen-angel pipeline. Watch the single-name CDS of the biggest AI-capex names as the canary.
**Resolves on:** Hyperscaler earnings (capex guidance vs. cloud/AI revenue), new-issue calendar reception, rating-agency methodology updates on capex intensity.

### Utilities / Power (the quiet AI trade)
**Debate:** Data-center power demand (+165% by 2030 per GS) means the biggest utility capex cycle in decades — is that a credit story (leverage up, spreads wider) or an equity story (regulated returns on a bigger rate base)?
**Side A:** Regulated recovery makes the debt safe; issue away. **Side B:** Execution risk, rate-case fights, and the sheer scale of borrowing widen the sector; plus energy-cost passthrough politics after the war.
**Resolves on:** Rate-case outcomes, data-center interconnection queues, issuance volumes.

### Energy (war windfall)
**Debate:** Producers printed money during the >$100 spike and buyers scrambled for non-Gulf supply (Canadian producers notably profited) — but crude is back near $70 post-deal. Do you buy the deleveraging story or fade the volatility?
**Side A:** Balance sheets got repaired at $100; credit is cleaner than it's been in years. **Side B:** The sector's spread compression assumed a war premium that's now gone; capex discipline erodes fast.
**Resolves on:** Oil path (deal durability), capex announcements, shareholder-return vs. debt-paydown mix.

### Consumer / Retail (the inflation loser)
**Debate:** 4.2% CPI with energy leading = real-income squeeze. Spreads haven't fully priced it.
**Side A:** Employment is solid (job gains keeping pace with workforce) so the consumer holds. **Side B:** Energy is a regressive tax; low-end consumer credit metrics (see ABS section) are already flashing; HY retail is the short.
**Resolves on:** Retail sales, earnings guidance, delinquency data.

### Real estate–adjacent / REITs
**Debate:** Office is the known disaster; the new question is data-center REITs — the market's favorite growth story, now with single-tenant concentration risk on AI counterparties whose own economics are unproven.
**Resolves on:** CMBS delinquency prints, data-center lease pricing, AI-tenant credit quality.

### Financials
**Debate:** Banks are earning well (prime brokerage and markets revenue booming — JPM equities +40% y/y in Q4'25 to $2.9bn, largely HF-driven) but are also the financing counterparty to record hedge-fund leverage (Hub 4). 
**Side A:** Best fundamentals in the index, tight for a reason. **Side B:** You're selling insurance on the leverage complex — bank spreads are where an HF de-gross event ultimately lands.
**Resolves on:** Prime-brokerage exposure disclosures, any vol event, regulatory noise on NBFI leverage.

### The index-level frame (for any credit person)
**Debate:** Spreads near cycle tights vs. a macro that includes 4% CPI, a possible hiking cycle, and a war. **Side A (technicals):** All-in yields are the story — insurance/pension demand is price-insensitive at 5–6% IG yields; every backup gets bought. **Side B (fundamentals):** You're not being paid for hike risk or the HY refi wall; single-name dispersion under a sleepy index is where the trade is.
**Question to ask a credit trader:** "Is the AI capex issuance wave a gift or a warning — and is your desk axed to buy or fade the new deals?"

## SECURITIZED — by subsector

### Agency MBS
**Debate:** Extension risk in a possibly-hiking world. With cuts pushed to 2027, mortgages stay outstanding longer; a hike extends duration further exactly when you don't want it (negative convexity at its worst).
**Side A:** Extension is priced; current-coupon spreads to Treasuries are historically attractive; the Fed's balance-sheet pivot (reinvesting MBS paydowns into Treasuries but no longer actively shrinking) removes a seller. Money managers overweight.
**Side B:** If the Fed hikes, the basis widens again — fast money short. Bank demand still structurally impaired vs. pre-2022.
**Resolves on:** The Fed path (same catalysts as rates), housing turnover, bank demand data.

### Non-agency RMBS
**Debate:** Housing credit is pristine (locked-in low rates, equity cushions) vs. affordability at generational lows. Mostly Side A wins here — the debate is really about supply scarcity keeping spreads tight.

### CMBS — split it in two
**Office:** The known workout story — the debate is whether the marks are finally real. Side A: distressed buyers stepping in, worst is priced. Side B: refi wall at current rates means the defaults are still ahead, extend-and-pretend is ending.
**Data centers (the growth engine):** Data-center securitization is one of the fastest-growing corners of the market. Debate: bulletproof AAA infrastructure cash flows vs. single-industry concentration on tenants (AI companies) that are themselves unprofitable and pre-IPO. If AI monetization stumbles, this is where securitized meets Hub 3 risk.
**Resolves on:** Special-servicing/delinquency data monthly, data-center lease renewals, AI-tenant financial disclosure (which the IPOs will force — see equities).

### CLOs
**Debate:** The loan market's weak tail vs. structural protection. Base rates at 3.50–3.75% mean floating-rate borrowers carry heavy interest burdens.
**Side A:** CLO structures have survived everything; AAA/AA spreads are free money; equity tranches benefit from high reinvestment spreads. **Side B:** CCC buckets creeping up, recovery rates on loan defaults structurally lower (loan-only capital structures), and Man Group just cut Structured Credit to negative — the smart money is trimming.
**Resolves on:** Loan default/recovery data, CCC bucket trends, downgrade/upgrade ratio, new-issue arbitrage math.

### Consumer ABS
**Debate:** The K-shaped consumer. Subprime auto delinquencies at/near record highs vs. prime performing fine.
**Side A:** It's a subprime-specific story, structures protect, senior tranches immune. **Side B:** It's the leading indicator — the energy-price squeeze hits the same low-end consumer first, and it spreads up the stack.
**Resolves on:** Monthly trust performance data (the cleanest real-time consumer read on Wall Street — worth saying to a securitized person, they'll agree).

### Esoteric ABS (data centers again, fiber, solar)
The frontier — where the AI and energy megatrends get securitized directly. Ask what deals they're seeing; the answer tells you where the buildout capital is actually coming from.
**Question to ask a securitized trader:** "Where in the stack are you comfortable being long the AI buildout — and is office finally cheap or still a falling knife?"

## CASH EQUITIES — by sector

### The tape context (say-able in one breath)
Solid economy, hawkish Fed, war premium unwinding, and an equity market wrestling with two things at once: whether higher-for-longer compresses multiples, and how to price the AI trade's *next phase* — the IPO wave.

### Semis / hardware (AI supply chain)
**Debate:** Capex sustainability. Every hyperscaler earnings call is a referendum on whether the buildout continues.
**Side A:** Order books are years deep; power, not demand, is the constraint; Cerebras just priced an upsized $5.6bn IPO and popped 68% day one — public-market appetite for AI hardware is proven.
**Side B:** The capex is being funded by companies (OpenAI: projected −$14bn losses in 2026, profitability ~2029–30) whose own economics are unproven; any capex-guidance cut cascades down the whole supply chain; positioning is maximally crowded (Hub 4 — Alphabet/Microsoft/Meta among the most crowded HF longs).
**Resolves on:** Hyperscaler capex guidance each quarter, the AI IPO reception (below), export-control headlines.

### SaaS / software (the disruption discount)
**Debate:** Does AI kill software or supercharge it? The market has voted harshly — Renaissance Capital's IPO strategist put it bluntly: the software sector generally *doesn't qualify* for this IPO window unless it can prove AI-resistance. Public SaaS multiples carry a persistent disruption discount.
**Side A (it's overdone):** Incumbents own the distribution, the data, and the workflow — they *are* the AI application layer; buy quality SaaS at discounted multiples.
**Side B (it's rational):** Seat-based pricing dies when agents do the work; the moat was workflow lock-in and AI dissolves it; the discount is permanent repricing, not sentiment. And the enterprise AI leaders (Anthropic ~80% enterprise revenue mix, reportedly leading LLM revenue share ~31%) are eating exactly the budgets SaaS lived on.
**Resolves on:** Net revenue retention prints, any incumbent's AI-revenue disclosure, and — bluntly — the LLM IPO S-1s, which will publish the first audited economics of the companies doing the disrupting.

### Telco (+ the connectivity angle)
**Debate:** Boring utility or AI infrastructure play? Fiber-to-data-center demand, backbone traffic growth, and edge buildout give telcos a growth story for the first time in a decade — but the sector's history is capex that never earns its cost of capital.
**Side A:** Real AI-driven demand for connectivity, undervalued vs. any infra comp, dividend-supported. **Side B:** The AI value accrues to the data center and the model, not the pipe — telcos build it and someone else monetizes it (the 5G lesson).
**Resolves on:** Enterprise/wholesale revenue mix in earnings, fiber pricing, capex-to-revenue trajectory.

### Oil & gas equities
**Debate:** The war made the case (energy security premium, windfall cash) and the peace deal is unwinding it (crude ~$70, and supply-heavy 2026 forecasts pre-dated the war — non-OPEC growth running well ahead of demand).
**Side A:** Balance sheets transformed, buybacks locked in, and the geopolitical optionality is free — any deal wobble is a call option you're paid to hold. **Side B:** Strip is fading, the structural bear case (supply growth 3x demand growth) reasserts, and generalists who bought the war trade are sellers on the way down.
**Resolves on:** Deal implementation, OPEC+ behavior, inventory data.

### SPOTLIGHT — the LLM IPO backlog (know this cold; it's the ECM story of the decade)
**The facts:** SpaceX went first — listed June 12, ~$85bn raised (largest IPO ever), popped ~19–20% day one, then slid from >$225 to ~$153. Anthropic filed confidentially June 1; last private round $965bn valuation; still reportedly tracking a late-2026 (as early as October) debut; ARR reported anywhere from $30bn to ~$44bn on explosive growth, ~80% enterprise, first operating profit reportedly in Q2'26, targeting breakeven ~2028. OpenAI filed about a week later; Altman reportedly pushing ~$1trn valuation and a ~$60bn raise; ~$25bn+ annualized revenue but projected −$14bn losses in 2026 and profitability ~2029–30; post-SpaceX wobble, reportedly weighing a delay to 2027. Combined mega-listing demand estimated at $100–200bn vs. $28.4bn total US IPO proceeds YTD — multiple times the record full-year 2021 ($156bn).
**Debate 1 — absorption/displacement:** Can the market take this much paper? Side A: $8trn sits in money-market funds; institutions have wanted pure-play AI exposure for years and only had proxies (NVDA, MSFT, GOOGL). Side B: the money rotates *out of* the Mag7 proxies to fund it — mechanically bearish the current index leadership; QQQ-tracking passive money literally must sell incumbents to make room on index inclusion. Watch MSFT and SoftBank specifically as OpenAI-proxy unwinds.
**Debate 2 — valuation:** ~22x sales for Anthropic, ~$1trn for a company losing $14bn/yr at OpenAI. Side A: fastest revenue growth in enterprise software history, land-grab economics. Side B: SpaceX's post-pop slide is the market's verdict on priced-for-perfection mega-listings; the S-1s force audited reality onto private-market marks.
**Debate 3 — sequencing game theory:** Anthropic filed first and appears willing to go first; OpenAI can wait, watch the reception, and price off it — but delay risks listing into a worse tape.
